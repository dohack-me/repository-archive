RSA

RSA (Rivest-Shamir-Adleman) is a widely used public-key encryption algorithm that allows for secure data transmission1. It relies on the difficulty of factoring a very large number into its prime factors, and uses concepts of primes and modular arithmetic.

Being an asymmetric encryption algorithm, RSA uses 2 different keys: a public key for encryption and a private key for decryption.

Key generation

  1. Two prime numbers ppp and qqq are chosen. These numbers are kept private.

  2. The modulus nnn is calculated through p∗qp * qp∗q. This is used in both the public and private key.

  3. ϕ\phiϕ (phi) is calculated through (p−1)∗(q−1)(p-1)*(q-1)(p−1)∗(q−1).

  4. The public exponent eee is chosen such that 1<e<ϕ1 < e < \phi1<e<ϕ and eee is coprime to ϕ\phiϕ (coprime means the two numbers share no common factors).

  5. The private exponent ddd is calculated as the modular multiplicative inverse of eee modulo ϕ\phiϕ.

The public key consists of nnn and eee while the private key consists of nnn and ddd. The public key will be distributed for encryption while the private key is hidden for decryption.

Encryption

  1. The plaintext MMM is converted into an integer mmm using a reversible protocol.

  2. The ciphertext ccc is calculated through memod  nm^e \mod nmemodn.

Decryption

  1. The ciphertext is decrypted through cdmod  nc^d \mod ncdmodn, which gives us mmm.

  2. From there we convert mmm back into the plaintext message.

Hence, ddd has to be kept hidden, but if we have access to ppp and qqq, we can calculate ddd and decrypt messages!
